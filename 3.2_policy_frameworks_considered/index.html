Three Key Frameworks in Policy Change

1. Advocacy Coalition Framework (ACF)

  • Coalitions formed around shared beliefs and interests
  • Compete to shape policy outcomes
  • Policy change driven by coalition learning and external events

Example: Environmental groups influencing climate policy.

2. Punctuated Equilibrium Theory (PET)

  • Long periods of stability punctuated by sudden, intense changes
  • Role of policy image and venue shopping
  • External shocks can disrupt the equilibrium

Example: Major policy changes following economic crises.

3. Multiple Streams Approach

  • Problem, Policy, and Politics streams
  • Policy windows: opportunities for change
  • Role of policy entrepreneurs in advancing issues

Example: Healthcare reform efforts during political shifts.

Incrementalism and Its Critique

Incrementalism and Bounded Rationality

  • Explains gradual policy changes and stability
  • Actors operate under bounded rationality
  • Policy changes through successive limited comparisons

Critique of Incrementalism

  • Inadequate for explaining radical policy shifts
  • Assumes a preference for small, incremental changes
  • May overlook opportunities for significant reforms
  • Doesn't stand up to a "Moonshot" challenge

Complexity of the Policy Process

  • Interplay of multiple actors and institutions
  • Difficulties in identifying clear causal relationships
  • Importance of using multiple frameworks for comprehensive understanding

Discussion: How do various frameworks help in understanding complex policy environments?

Power Dynamics in Policy

  • Three faces of power: Coercive, Blocking, Quiescence
  • How these faces affect agenda-setting and policy change
  • Examples of each face in action

Activity: Discuss student term paper policy topic issues and identify which of the the three faces of power were at play.

Critical Review of Frameworks

  • Insights and limitations of each framework
  • Complementary nature of different approaches
  • Importance of a multifaceted view of policymaking

Takeaway: No single framework can fully capture the complexity of the policy process.
